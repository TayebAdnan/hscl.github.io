Innovative Additive Manufacturing (3D Printing) of Composites

Additive Manufacturing (AM), known as 3-D printing using direct 3-D CAD data, enables the fast and cost-efficient production, reduces material usage and part counts, and shortens design cycles mainly due to recent development of thermoplastic materials and process of AM, applicable not only to prototypes but also to functional products and tooling.

Figure 1. Possible application of Additive Manufacturing to an composite fan blade (e.g. GE): a complex composite tooling by Fusion Deposition Modeling (FDM) and a leading edge protecting fan blade by direct metal laser deposition (DMLD) (TWI Ltd, UK).


Tooling for Composite Structures
A major portion of the overall cost of current composite manufacturing is typically composed tooling and molds that involves expensive and complex machining. It is also requiring skilled workers and long lead times to meet design specifications. Tooling and molds can be manufactured in an automated process directly from a CAD file as featured in AM, offering shorter lead times, lower expense and higher accuracy. It is already well demonstrated that, among advanced AM, Fusion Deposition Modeling (FDM) can be well used to rapidly and cost-effectively produce tooling for composites especially for complex parts. Thermoplastics for FDM have been developed suitable for high temperature tooling for polymer reinforced composites.

However, among other properteis, the Coefficient of Thermal Expansion (CTE) of such thermoplastics are much higher than conventional aluminium tools, and it can be critical to the final shape of the parts. The Computer-Aided Engineering (CAE) software should be used to analyze thermal and mechanical behavior of such tooling on the final products. In order to increase durability of molds, prototypes can be manufactured from AM, and then molds can be fabricated using these prototypes.

Figure 2. Aluminum tooling and molds may significantly delay design cycles (courtesy of Koenigsegg); molds for out-of-autoclave prepreg carbon fiber (easy composites); tooling and molds for composites parts by AM (Stratasys); a complicated tool for composites (Airtech).

Carbon Fiber Reinforced Thermoplastics for Selective Laser Sintering
The products out of the Additive Manufacturing are now more than prototypes, providing high stiffness and strength for real application as required in aerospace and automobile industry. Prototypes created using additive fabrication processes such as selective laser sintering (SLS) and stereolithography have been and are still being used in the product design and development process to check form, fit and function to varying degrees. Until recently, the prototyping materials allowed for the creation of prototypes that were good enough for testing but were not fully suited for final use. With the latest advanced materials used with additive manufacturing in general and SLS in particular, final production parts can be fully functional. A range of selective laser sintering materials (e.g. metal, glass or carbon fiber-filled plastics) are engineered to meet an array of defense, aerospace, medical, industrial and consumer applications.

However, due to an orientation of the fibers in each layer the mechanical properties can vary in the three different directions, ranging 2 to 8 GPa in stiffness and 30 to 80 MPa in strength. The vertical direction to the layered plane (z-direction) shows weakest properties. The effects of material mixing ratios and the sintering layer direction on the mechanical performance of the final products can be analyzed using CAE tools: Integrated CAD, CAE and AM can maximize the mechanical performance as well as achieve the competitive manufacturing process.

Figure3. Carbon-fiber filled Nylon for Selective Laser Sintering (NW rapid mfg, http://www.nwrapidmfg.com); Functional Parts that are durable, heat and chemical resistant result from utilizing the SLS process (http://www.solidconcepts.com).

To further enhance the strength and stiffness of the Additive Manufactured structures, thermoplastics (e.g. polymer matrix polyamide) can be reinforced with nanocomposite or carbon precursor fibers. In order to establish file format to convey precise material content information between the CAD and the printing machines, a project is undertaken in National Institute of Standard and Test to update the conventional STL codes.
Figure 4. Nanofiber strengthening in Fusion Deposition Molding, Chad Duty, ORNL, DoE (http://cfcomposites.org)

In order to make the products even lighter, stronger and more competitive, industry needs to perform more accurate, realistic and reliable prediction of strength and fatigue life of composites in the design and manufacturing process. Among available methods, micromechanics based stress and fatigue analysis of the fiber reinforced composites are very efficient in estimating the structural stiffness, strengths and fatigue life under real loading conditions and in studying the effects of fiber contents and layering directions on the final products.
Figure 5. Multiscale analysis of short fiber composites: statistical fiber distribution is included to study the strength, stiffness and fatigue life prediction of AM products. (http://composites.hanyang.ac.kr)

Robot assisted continuous fiber placement or tape layering manufacturing
Automated manufacturing of composites can be also categorized as “Additive Manufacturing” since fibers are added or layered over configured molds or tools as in Automated Tapes Layering (ATL) or Automated Fiber Placing (AFP). Similarly to AM, AFP/ATL programming software also reads CAD surface and ply boundary information and adds material according to user-specified manufacturing standards and requirements. Depending on the complexity and size of structural geometry, robots with required motional axes are used, and are programmed based on the CAD and laminate sequence.

The conventional fiber tows and unidirectional tapes are fed to the robot arm in AFP/ATL and the motion can be very complex depending on the configuration and the layup sequence. Using newly developed multidirectional tapes (C-ply from Chomarat), the robot motion can be dramatically simplified and material usages can be reduced. According to the size and layup sequence of the laminated structures, users can specify the thickness of ply and width of multidirectional tapes, affecting mechanical performance of the final product. Recently, thermoplastics can be rapidly consolidated for pipes and curved structures. It is expected that rapid consolidated thermoplastic composites is the key technology to advanced AM of composites. The multidirectional tapes can be also utilized to the laser assisted processing of fiber-reinforced thermoplastic composites.

New disruptive 3-D paths, instead of sliced 2-D data used in current AM, can be developed for depositing fibers, resins and possibly third materials for backing immediate. The approach may eliminate tools, as noticed in metal laser deposition, featuring less material scrap, shorter design cycle, and much reduced part count.

Figure 6. Laser assisted processing of fiber-reinforced thermoplastic composite; Fiber placement robots.

Cellular Digital Materials
As one of “Additive manufacturing”, Interlocking cellular digital materials have been proposed in building a large scale composite structures. Cellular materials are made of composites, and are assembled, dissembled and resembled as needed. Theoretically, the stiffness and strength per density can be 10 times higher than conventional composite structures. This approach of assembled manufacturing rather than layered or printed is really disruptive.
